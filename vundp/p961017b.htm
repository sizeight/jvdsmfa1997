CITIES JOIN UN DEVELOPMENT PROGRAMME IN DRIVE AGAINST POVERTY

40 North American Cities Proclaim 17 October Poverty Eradication Day

Embargoed: Thursday, 17 October at 12:00 noon EDT

Note to Reporters: James Gustave Speth, UNDP Administrator, will address the Daily Press Briefing of the United Nations Secretary-General's Spokesman. The event will take place in Room 226 of the UN Secretariat, immediately following the 12 noon briefing. UN accreditation is required.

New York, 17 October 1996 -- The fight to stop poverty, which afflicts some 1.3 billion people worldwide, has united mayors from cities across the USA and Canada. They are seeking to draw widespread public attention to the harsh effects of social deprivation and to help rally both local and international support against it.

Mobilized by the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) and the association Sister Cities International, mayors from 40 cities in North America have signed proclamations declaring 17 October Poverty Eradication Day in their municipalities.

Cities joining the drive against poverty include: Akron, Ohio; Albuquerque, New Mexico; Arlington and Austin, Texas; Charlotte, North Carolina; Cleveland, Ohio; Colombus, Georgia; Denver, Colorado; Detroit, Michigan; Durham, North Carolina; Edmonton, Alberta (Canada); Escondido, California; Fort Wayne, Indiana; Honolulu, Hawaii; Jersey City, New Jersey; Kansas City, Missouri; Lincoln, Nebraska; Louisville, Kentucky; Miami, Florida; Minneapolis, Minnesota; Nashville, Tennessee; New Haven, Connecticut; New York, New York; Oklahoma City, Oklahoma; Omaha, Nebraska; Philadelphia, Pennsylvania; Raleigh, North Carolina; Richmond, Virginia; San Francisco, San Jose and Santa Clarita, California; St. Paul, Minnesota; Spokane, Washington; Stockton, California; Syracuse, New York; Tampa, Florida; Tempe, Arizona; Tucson, Arizona; Tulsa, Oklahoma and Washington, DC.

"Poverty does not only affect women and children living in developing countries; poor (people) live in cities in the most industrialized countries, including Philadelphia and many other cities here in the United States," said Edward G. Rendell, Mayor of Philadelphia. "To become a truly great global community, we must re-emphasize our commitment to efforts to eradicate poverty and its devastating impact on human well-being."

The proclamation signed by Mayor Rendell and others reads in part: "This occasion is an opportunity for us to rededicate ourselves to make the fight against poverty an absolute priority, focusing on the needs of the poor and upon measures that are being undertaken with the collaboration of the city government and its partners to eradicate poverty...."

In industrialized countries, including the US, more than 100 million people live in poverty and five million are homeless, according to the Human Development Report 1996, published by Oxford University Press for UNDP. And, while the US enjoys steady economic and job growth, the number of people living below the poverty line is actually growing. That is why the report states that poverty eradication efforts should not be limited to poor countries. Poverty extends beyond national borders, just as it extends beyond the confines of the inner city, the report states.

Reacting to the urgency of the poverty challenge, the United Nations General Assembly designated 17 October the "International Day for the Eradication of Poverty" and 1996 the "International Year for the Eradication of Poverty". The mobilization of North American mayors is one of a series of public awareness activities planned by UNDP for the week of 17 October through 24 October.

Other activities planned include: a UNDP-United Nations Association contest aimed at encouraging school children across the US to take up poverty eradication projects in their own communities; and, in what is billed as a decisive gesture of solidarity for the world's poor, UNDP has invited staff of all UN agencies to fast on 18 October and to donate $5 each to development projects. Additional information on UNDP's poverty eradication activities may be found on UNDP's home page on the World Wide Web (http://www.undp.org).

"Unless we address the problems of poverty now, none of the great goals the international community has set -- peace, stability, human rights for all, preservation of the environment -- are achievable in a world where half of the people find themselves shut out of opportunity and the benefits of a global society," said UNDP Administrator James Gustave Speth.
