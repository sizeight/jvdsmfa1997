URBAN FARMING HELPS COMBAT POVERTY, MALNUTRITION

One-Seventh of World's Food Produced in Garbage Dumps, Vacant Lots, on Rooftops, Other Urban Locales

800 Million Urban Farmers Worldwide

NEW YORK, 13 February 1996 -- Urban farming is helping lift hundreds of millions of people out of extreme poverty and is improving health and nutrition of city-dwellers across the globe, states a report published by the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) today.

Urban agriculture is practiced by an estimated 800 million people, shows the report, Urban Agriculture: Food, Jobs and Sustainable Cities. It takes place in all regions of the world--in developing and developed countries alike--but is most prevalent in Asia.

Urban agriculture ranges from growing crops on rooftops to raising livestock in backyards and raising fish in ponds, streams and lagoons.

For the poorest of the poor, urban agriculture provides access to food and helps stamp out malnutrition, says the report. For the "stable poor," it provides a source of income and high-quality food at a low cost. And for middle-income families, it offers the possibility of savings and a return on investment in urban property.

According to the report, the world's poorest urban households spend as much as 90 percent of their income on food. For these people, urban agriculture offers an opportunity for a better diet and a chance to shift household spending toward other needs, such as health care and housing. In Sao Paulo, Brazil, urban households--rich and poor--devote about 50 percent of their income to food. The situation is worse in Istanbul, Turkey, where 60 percent goes to food. In Lima, Peru, the number climbs to 70 percent, and in Ho Chi Minh City, Viet Nam, the number is as high as 80 percent.

Urban agriculture, which is on the rise everywhere as the populations of the world's cities swell, is also helping create jobs. In Calcutta, India, for example, about 20,000 people have found jobs farming on the city's garbage dumps.

"Urban farming provides scarce jobs to many in the city," the report says. "In some cities, as many as one-fifth to two-thirds of all families are engaged in agriculture, with as many as a third of these having no other source of income."

"Urban farming is a competitive economic activity and the industry of choice of millions of urban entrepreneurs," the study says. "It also provides income-generating opportunities for people with low skills and little capital, as well as people with limited mobility, including women, children and aged persons."

But benefits of urban agriculture extend beyond better nutrition, poverty reduction and jobs for the poor. Environmental benefits are equally significant, the report notes.

"Urban agriculture turns waste from a problem into a resource, reduces the public cost of waste management...and provides a better living environment." In Khartoum, for example, about one-fourth of the city's garbage is consumed by farm animals. In Calcutta, sewage feeds 3,000 hectares of lagoons, which produce 6,000 tons of fish each year. And in the State of California, 200 wastewater reclamation plants save nearly 800,000 cubic meters of water a day, with most of the treated effluent put to agricultural use.

The news is not all good, however. Urban agriculture does have a downside, the report warns. When untreated human waste is used as fertilizer, consumers risk contracting diseases like cholera and hepatitis. And indiscriminate use of pesticides and chemical fertilizers can contaminate local water supplies.

Too often, governments have confronted these problems by attempting to ban urban agriculture, denying cities an important source of food and economic growth. A better solution is to devise policies that encourage urban farmers to keep producing food yet protect people from key health hazards, the report argues.

Because governments have often failed to nurture urban agriculture or to understand how it works, its economic potential remains largely untapped. "It has become clear that urban agriculture has been underrated in all but a handful of countries--and mostly overlooked by the international development community," writes UNDP Assistant Administrator Anders Wijkman in the foreword of the report. But by putting simple laws into effect, urban agriculture's potential can be unleashed and the sector can become a formidable economic force in the 21st century.

The report is based on three years of research funded mostly by UNDP and carried out mainly by the Urban Agriculture Network, a Washington-based non-governmental organization.

The report is the first in a series of publications to be released by UNDP in 1996 to guide the debate at the United Nations-sponsored HABITAT-II conference to take place in Istanbul in June.
