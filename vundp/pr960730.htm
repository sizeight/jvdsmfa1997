OLYMPIC MEDALLISTS JOIN INTERNATIONAL APPEAL AGAINST POVERTY

Appeal launched in Atlanta by United Nations Development Programme and International Olympic Committee

Atlanta, 30 July 1996 -- Dozens of Olympic medal winners have pledged to support a global drive against poverty by signing an appeal launched by UNDP and the International Olympic Committee (IOC). A massive billboard in Atlanta's Olympic Village that posts the text of the appeal has been signed by thousands of athletes. The medallists include:

Dominique Dawes, USA, gymnastics (gold/bronze); Shannon Miller, USA, gymnastics (gold); Charles Austin, USA, high jump (gold); Brad Bridgewater, USA, swimming (gold); John Hargis, USA, swimming (gold); Marie Jose Perec, France, 400m (gold); Claudia Poll, Costa Rica, swimming (gold); Jefferson Perez, Ecuador, 200 km walk (gold); Saphir Suleymanoglu, Turkey, weightlifting (gold); Tripp Schwenk, USA, swimming (gold/silver); Irina Gerasimenok, Russia, shooting (silver); Frankie Fredericks, Namibia, 100m (silver); Paul Tergat, Kenya, 10,000m (silver); Ana Fidelia Quirot, Cuba, 800m (silver) Giles Bosquet/Bertrand Vectan, France, rowing (silver). Maria Mutola, Mozambique, 800 meters (bronze); Salah Hissou, Morocco, 10,000m (bronze); Frederic Kowal, France, rowing (bronze).

Other athletes who have joined the appeal, from the US, include Grant Hill, basketball, Kelly Blair, heptathlon, and Sheila Hudson, triple jump. Ethiopian track stars Haile Gebrselassie and Derartu Tulu have also added their names to the drive against poverty.

One in five people lives in absolute poverty today, 900 million adults cannot read or write and 1.3 billion people do not have access to safe water. Pledging to join the battle against poverty reflects the Olympic ideals of human dignity and well-being. The UN General Assembly proclaimed 1997-2006 as the International Decade for the Eradication of Poverty, following the International Year for the Eradication of Poverty, 1996. The appeal launched by the IOC and UNDP coincides with these drives.

Athletes who sign up pledge to help fight poverty, promote solidarity and human development. They also seek to promote development, rally public opinion and call for national and international actions as they request societies to make the fight against poverty an absolute priority. Already, national Olympic committees and UNDP offices in 40 countries have begun collaborating to promote greater access to sports by poor people, as well as new projects to combat poverty at the local level.

Upon signing the large billboard in Olympic Village, Moses Kiptanui, the 3,000 meter steeplechase contender from Kenya, said that poverty is killing human progress, and makes people fight each other. "People should be exposed to different societies and sports is the best way to get people together. It is not my will to become a great athlete. My will is to help people through sports. I am running for them, and I only feel useful if I can do something for the poor communities in my country," he said.

Other Kenyan Olympic team members who have signed the appeal include Paul Bitok, Joseph Gykonio, Linu Kimutai and Vincent Malakwan. From Switzerland, the cyclist Alex Zulle has joined the appeal, as has the Spanish marathon runner Rocio Rios and Colombian high jump contender Gilmar Mayo.

"This appeal comes at a time when poverty has become the greatest challenge facing the planet," said the Administrator of UNDP, James Gustave Speth. "It endangers the future of all our children and of coming generations. Drastic measures are needed at national and international levels to avoid carrying this deprivation well into the next century." The fight to end poverty is also a matter of individual civic responsibility, Mr. Speth said. This is why UNDP and the IOC are developing a joint programme of action to stimulate grassroots initiatives throughout the world and to inform the public about the nature of poverty and solutions.
