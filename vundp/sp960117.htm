Stop the Clock!

United Nations Secretariat, New York, 17 January 1996

Special Event in Support of the International Year for the Eradication of Poverty

Mr. James Gustave Speth

I'm pleased that so many of you are able to join us today for this event, which is both a cause for alarm and optimism.

Alarm because we are turning on a poverty clock which will remind us that each day another 67,000 people join the ranks of the poor. That's about 25 million people a year, most of whom will be in the developing world. Seven out of 10 will be female.

Optimism because the international community now recognizes the urgency of this problem, realises what must be done and is taking steps to halt, indeed reverse, this grim trend.

The job ahead of us is monumental. Not only do we have the task of raising the incomes of the 1.3 billion people living on less than $1 a day, we also face the daunting task of creating greater equity, opportunity and empowerment.

Last March in Copenhagen, at the United Nations Social Summit, world leaders came together to acknowledge the worsening problem of global poverty, deplore its spread and commit themselves to actions to reverse this trend. Never before have so many heads of state and government joined hands to reach a consensus on building a poverty-free world.

The agreements reached at the Social Summit are revolutionary in their scope, complexity and urgency. And they highlight the key role to be played by people-centred development in eradicating poverty.

That's where the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) comes in. We're about development that protects and regenerates the environment, creates jobs and income-opportunities, gives women a chance to be full partners in society and, above all, helps reduce the incidence and impact of poverty.

We are committed to creating a strong enabling environment that supports economic growth; gives priority to the poor, to jobs and to sustainable livelihoods; and encourages participation, tolerance and respect for human diversity.

In the battle against poverty, UNDP has taken many important steps forward since the Social Summit. I'd like to name a few:

· With the support of the national governments that are members of UNDP, we've made poverty-eradication our overarching goal.

· We're working with developing countries to build or strengthen national poverty-eradication strategies with time-bound targets for progress.

· And, at our headquarters here in New York and in all of our offices worldwide, we've increased the number of staff working on poverty issues and projects.

We must not accept that the poor will always be with us. The world has the material and natural resources, the know-how and the people to make a poverty-free world a reality. And until the Social Summit last March, all that was lacking was the global commitment.

This is more than just a dream. One hundred sixty years ago the world launched a crusade against slavery. Most people were sceptical. But there was a will to change. Today, I call upon you to launch a similar crusade -- for a world without poverty.

Let me conclude with a comment about the clock. We see unrelenting growth in the numbers. But we must never forget that behind every tick of the clock, there is a face, a person, a life. We're talking about people. In the end, that's what the International Year for the Eradication of Poverty is all about.

Our goal for today, for the entire Year and beyond, is to maintain the momentum gained in Copenhagen and to build upon it. We must search for innovative ways to transform the concepts presented and the resolutions reached at the Social Summit into a positive reality that lifts people out of poverty, makes a difference in their lives and -- more than anything else -- helps stop this clock.
