THREE WHO ARE BEATING THE POVERTY CLOCK

Winning the fight against poverty with help from friends

The United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) invited these three people, whose lives have changed (and who in turn are changing the lives of others), to share their experiences at an event held at UN Headquarters in New York to inaugurate the International Year for the Eradication of Poverty.

Following are their brief biographies and descriptions of the projects in which they are involved. Betty Bangira is a Zimbabwean mother of four whose life was changed when her community joined with five others to create an irrigation project in one of the country's desertified provinces. She has been elected spokesperson of her cooperative for her commitment to self-help against poverty.

Luis Ticona Mamani is an indigenous Aymara of the Bolivian highlands who is now the Director of an association of alpaca herdsmen. The association has improved both the quality and exports of alpaca fibre and has tripled incomes of some 4,000 families in Bolivia's barren altiplano.

Lenore Cola is a mother of three, a resident of the Harlem section of New York, USA. Ms. Cola, whose family was homeless for four years, became involved in 1989 in a street library programme for children and their parents organized by International Movement ATD Fourth World, and has since become a leader in the programme and in her community.

Betty Bangira

Betty Bangira is married with four children and lives in the Mhondoro Communal Area in Bangira Village about 80 kilometres south of Harare in Mashonaland West Province, Zimbabwe. Her husband, who suffers from a kidney ailment, has been unemployed for more than five years. Ms. Bangira is 27 years old and has two years of secondary school education. She lives in a hut and works one half of a hectare of vegetables. The nearest borehole for safe drinking water is five kilometres away. Her livestock consists of two chickens. Ms. Bangira is a member of the Kushinga Cooperative.

As in many Communal Areas in Zimbabwe, the Mhondoro Communal Area suffers from encroaching desertification. A group of people from six villages approached the United Nations Development Programme's "Africa 2000" programme in 1993 for help in irrigating the low-quality, dry land of the area. A dam was built by 1995 which provided necessary irrigation. Ms. Bangira joined the cooperative in 1995. Although it was a bad drought year, she was able to grow and harvest vegetables, and with the proceeds buy mealie meal, two pots and a sugar bowl. Next harvest she wants to buy a pan. Her colleagues selected her to be their ambassador of goodwill for her thrift, her work with others and her commitment to self-help against poverty.

Mhondoro Communal Area Irrigation Project,
Zimbabwe

This project combines poverty alleviation, natural resource management, food production and biodiversity.

Background

The Mhondoro Communal Area of Zimbabwe suffers from encroaching desertification resulting from widespread soil erosion due in turn to deforestation, uncontrolled cultivation and overgrazing. Land degradation and gullies are common. Rivers in the area are silted, and the occurrence of drought, even on a small scale, results in severe livestock losses and food shortages.

The project

Six villages in the Mashonaland West Province of Zimbabwe organized themselves into the Kushinga Cooperative in October 1993 and approached the Africa 2000 Network of the United Nations Development Programme. Africa 2000 supports income-generating and environmentally sound small projects in rural Africa, designed and put in place by the people who benefit from them. Grants are typically less than US$5,000. Half of the national coordinators are women. The Kushinga Cooperative wanted funding of a water catchment basin for irrigation. The objectives: improved range lands for increased productivity of livestock; better management of arable lands to reduce soil erosion and enhance crop yields; increased water supply to permit introduction of additional plant species in the environment; increased tree planting, and a community more responsible for its own development and environmental management.

A small dam was constructed between May of 1994 and June of 1995 with the assistance of agricultural engineering and environmental technical services of the government. The capacity of the dam is 30,400 cubic metres, or 6.7 million gallons. The community contribution in labor, tree planting and reclamation of gullies is estimated at $4,120. The resulting projects include a grazing scheme and nutrition garden, managed by the Cooperative.

The outside funding for the project, $50,000, was committed by UNDP/Africa 2000 and the Small Grants programme of the Global Environment Facility (GEF). GEF is a joint funding and technical assistance organization for environmentally sustainable development managed by UNDP, the World Bank and the United Nations Environment Programme.

Luis Ticona Mamani

Luis Ticona was born into an indigenous Aymara family and community on the altiplano of Bolivia in 1946. He has devoted all his working life to improving the economic condition of the impoverished rural families of the Bolivian highlands.

Mr. Ticona was trained in community development, cooperative enterprise management and accounting. He began his career in 1969 with the National Community Development Service, where he worked for five years in remote rural areas. His successes there led to his appointment as national coordinator of programs for rural youth.

In 1979, moved by the plight of impoverished alpaca producers of the altiplano, Mr. Ticona and several of his community development colleagues developed a project to improve the economic situation of the families whose only source of income is the raising of the alpaca (a "camelid," related to the llama) and the sale of the alpaca wool fibre. Mr. Ticona's work led to the establishment of the Integrated Association of Camelid Producers of the High Andes. Mr. Ticona has been the manager of the association nearly continuously since its founding, testimony to the high regard in which he is held by the more than 1,000 member families who have benefited from his stewardship of the association and its assets. He was recently elected president of the new National Network of Camelid Producers and was appointed to the National Livestock Policy Committee of the Bolivian Secretariat of Agriculture.

Bolivia Alpaca Fibre Production and
Processing Project

The Bolivia Alpaca Project, begun in 1992, was designed to alleviate the extreme poverty of approximately 4,000 alpaca producing families of the Bolivian Altiplano. Because of harsh conditions and high altitude these families depend on their alpacas (members of the same "camelid" family as llamas) as their only source of income. Prior to the project, family income averaged US$300 a year. Now it averages $900 a year. Before the project, poor quality alpaca wool fibre was bought by middlemen from neighbouring countries and smuggled out of Bolivia. Today, thanks to a $1.3 million processing plant opened in November of 1994 and 80 per cent owned by small alpaca producers, quality alpaca fibre is exported to Italian and other European spinning plants, as well as being used in the domestic knitted apparel industry.

Other results of the project:

  • Loans to 441 producer families with an on-time repayment rate of 98 per cent over the past four years.
  • The establishment of preventive and curative animal health programmes which have resulted in a 30 per cent reduction in adult alpaca mortality and 35 per cent reduction in infant alpaca deaths. The introduction of more than 7,000 high-quality breeding animals to improve the yields of 500 herds.
  • The development of 7,200 hectares of improved pastures and the irrigation and reclamation of 3,100 hectares of desertified land.

These results were attained through a field technical assistance programme carried out with the Association of Camelid Producers of the High Andes through hands-on extension services on farms, and a revolving loan fund. Technical assistance was provided in animal health, pasture improvement, genetic improvement, herd management and the introduction of modern shearing and fibre handling processes.

The processing plant is now producing 100 tons of alpaca wool a year, valued at $1.3 million annually on the European market. Firm orders from European textile companies have meant that producers are selling the fibre at $5 a kilogram to the plant, compared to $1.60 when sold to intermediaries in earlier years. The plant in turn adds value of $8 to $10 per kilogram, resulting in an export price of $13 to $15 a kilogram, some nine times the earlier top price received. Because the small producers hold 80 per cent stock ownership of the plant, either directly or through the association, the income from the plant can help make the entire industry self-sustaining. Proceeds from the plant will fund the veterinary and technical assistance activities begun under the project.

Funding for the project came from the producers association ($240,000), the United Nations Capital Development Fund ($3,135,623) the United Nations Development Programme ($683,288) and the organization Appropriate Technology International, or ATI ($442,625). ATI was the implementing organization in the project.

Lenore Cola

Ms. Lenore Cola was born in New Orleans, Louisiana in 1952. She was raised in New York City. She is currently living in Harlem with her three children.

Ms. Cola is a high-school graduate. She completed a year of nursing school, but was unable to finish the programme in which she was enroled.

She worked as a nursing assistant for seven years in Louisiana, then moved back to New York City. Unable to find a job, she became homeless. She and her family lived in different shelters for four years.

Ms. Cola became involved with the Fourth World Movement in 1989, while she was living in one of the shelters in Harlem. She and her children participated actively in the street library program run by Fourth World Movement volunteers in the area.

After having been rehoused, Ms. Cola became involved with the Fourth World Family Network. This training program brings together parents from poor and non poor backgrounds. One of its objectives is to encourage parents to support and prolong the activities of the street libraries. Another objective is to make known the lives, hopes and experiences of poor families. Parents thus learn together how to represent their own community. Members of the network have met with local officials and have participated in national and international meetings. Ms. Cola, in particular, has also helped new families become involved in this programme. In addition, she is active in her community, including within her children's school.

In October 1994, Ms. Cola was a delegate to the Second International Fourth World Family Congress, which took place in New York and in Washington, DC. Within the context of the congress, she participated in a seminar entitled, "Extreme Poverty and the Denial of Human Rights," organized by the United Nations Center for Human Rights. There, she actively took part in discussions with United Nations officials, scholars and other delegates to the congress from around the world.

The Street Library Programme

In a report to the French Economic and Social Council about extreme poverty and the insecurities to which it exposes people, the founder of the International Movement ATD Fourth World, Father Joseph Wresinski, wrote: "Many of the difficulties children encounter in school are rooted in their social, economic and, above all, cultural deprivation. One of the highest prorities in the fight against poverty is to improve access to basic education and skills training for the most disadvantaged children." The street library programme that is carried out with impoverished families in different parts of the world, developed as a way to foster an interest in learning through books, art and computers.

The local programme presently reaches about 120 children between the ages of 5 and 12, and their parents, in East New York, Brooklyn. In the past, it has been present in central Harlem, Coney Island, the South Bronx and in several shelters for homeless families. Volunteers of the International Movement ATD Fourth World and friends of the organization go to each site once a week after school with a bookcase on wheels and other material. Children come to read, to listen to stories and to participate in other activities that encourage them to learn. One long-term project last year involved constructing with the children a rollercoaster for marbles, inspired by a sculpture in the Port Authority. The workshop included designing the model on computer, and building it with a variety of tools and materials.

While keeping the focus on books, the programme introduces arts, crafts and computers. By discovering the joy of reading and learning in general, the children gain confidence in themselves. One child stated, "I like to read, it makes my brain good!"

The street library programme takes place where the children live, so their parents can play a role as active partners. These parents are confronted daily with the struggle to bring up their families in very difficult conditions. Because volunteers of ATD Fourth World want to learn from the parents what they already do to encourage their children to move ahead, they visit the parents and ask them to help plan and evaluate the activities of the street library. The project brings parents together and supports them in their desire to contribute to the improvement of their neighbourhoods. For example, some parents have been especially helpful in trying to get other, more excluded families to participate in the activities. One mother stated, "The volunteers help us organize. We try to get more parents and more kids involved. But it is kind of hard, because some parents don't want to come. They feel ashamed. They are afraid that somebody might look down on them. But in the street libraries it's not like that."

The International Movement ATD Fourth World tries to extend this partnership to include local government agencies and other members of the private sector that share the same concerns. For example, the street library programme has played a role in the city's struggle against the presence of drugs in its neighborhoods. The State of New York recognized this impact by presenting the organization with a grant from the Office of Alchoholism and Substance Abuse Services.
