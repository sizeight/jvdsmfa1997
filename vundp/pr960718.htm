OLYMPIC ATHLETES UNITE AGAINST POVERTY; APPEAL LAUNCHED IN ATLANTA BY INTERNATIONAL OLYMPIC COMMITTEE AND UNITED NATIONS DEVELOPMENT PROGRAMME

New York, 18 July 1996 -- Hundreds of athletes have signed a huge billboard at Atlanta's Olympic Village as part of the appeal against poverty that has been launched by the International Olympic Committee (IOC) and the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP). The appeal and the billboard signing demonstrate the athletes' strong commitment to help mobilize the international community against the scourge of poverty, which grips much of the world today. Currently, one in five people in the world lives in absolute poverty, 900 million adults cannot read or write and 1.3 billion people do not have access to safe water. Pledging to join the battle against poverty reflects the Olympic ideals of human dignity and well-being.

The United Nations General Assembly proclaimed 1997-2006 as the international decade for the eradication of poverty, following the International Year for the Eradication of Poverty (IYEP), 1996.

The IOC and UNDP are pooling their respective networks and capabilities to undertake grassroots anti-poverty initiatives and information campaigns on poverty around the world. The collaboration between the two institutions includes promoting greater access by poor people to sports and physical education.

In March, the IOC Athletes Commission pledged its full support to IOC humanitarian activities in general, and within the framework of the IYEP as proclaimed by the UN General Assembly in particular. It also called upon the athletes of the world "to actively participate in human development projects with the UN system and non-governmental organizations", as well as to be involved in promoting the well-being of society, including socially constructive initiatives and assisting the medically disadvantaged.

In line with this goal, the Appeal of the Olympic Athletes against Poverty launched in Atlanta starts with a commitment. Athletes who sign up pledge to help eradicate poverty, promote solidarity and human development. They also seek to rally public opinion, and call for national and international tangible action as they request that our societies make the fight against poverty an absolute priority.

"This accord comes at a time when poverty has become the greatest challenge facing the planet," said the Administrator of UNDP, James Gustave Speth. "Poverty on such a wide scale endangers the future of all our children and of coming generations. Drastic measures must be taken both at national and international levels if we are to avoid carrying this deprivation well into the next century. Forty billion dollars a year are needed to meet basic human development needs. Much more is needed to defeat poverty completely." Mr. Speth added that the fight to end poverty is also a matter of individual civic responsibility. This is why UNDP and the IOC are developing a joint programme of action to stimulate grassroots initiatives throughout the world, as well as to inform the public about the nature of the problem and its solutions.

It is expected that after the Olympic Games athletes will hand over the Appeal to the government authorities of their respective countries. "The goal of Olympism is to place everywhere sport at the service of the harmonious development of man, with a view to encouraging the establishment of a peaceful society concerned with the preservation of human dignity," stressed Juan Antonio Samaranch, President of the IOC, on the occasion of the signing of the IOC-UNDP cooperation agreement earlier this year. Mr. Samaranch also said that this cooperation "is part of the notion of Olympic Solidarity", noting that through the Olympic Solidarity fund, the IOC is also conducting development projects throughout the world.

In addition to the Appeal being launched at the Olympic Village during the XXVIth Games, field activities undertaken jointly by National Olympic Committees and local UNDP offices are beginning to get underway in developing countries throughout the world, including in Botswana, Colombia, Georgia and Malaysia.
