Putting Mass Poverty Behind Us: An Imperative for United Nations Cooperation

United Nations, Geneva, 15 May 1996

Press Conference

Mr. James Gustave Speth

Despite all efforts, poverty today is growing faster than population, or, at least not slower, and populations are growing in unprecedented numbers. One and a half billion people live in poverty in United Nations programme countries. The conditions of life of twice that number are deplorable. Half the people in sub-Sahara Africa will be living in absolute poverty by the year 2000.

Poverty is also relative, and here the figures are equally shocking. Between 1960 and 1990, the gap between the world's richest 20 per cent and the poorest 20 per cent did not decrease; it doubled — from 30 fold to 60 fold. Even with all the growth in Asia, the percentage of world income of the OECD countries actually increased, from 68 per cent to 72 per cent.

Without sustained progress to a fairer, more equitable world, growing poverty and joblessness, population pressures, environmental decay, and the widening gap between the haves and the have-nots will breed despair and instability. It is from this cauldron of injustice, frustration and hopelessness that conflict and violence boil over.

Any attack on mass poverty must include real economic growth, and the fundamental policies needed to assure it. But it is a myth that this growth is being widely achieved. The World Bank recently reported that over half of United Nations programme countries had negative real per capita income growth over the last decade. The 48 least developed countries (LDCs) are worst off. With 10 per cent of the world's population, they have 0.1 per cent of the world's income. For 25 years, their per capita incomes, on average, have declined. Today, it stands at about US$350.

It is also a myth that the forces of the private market in an increasingly globalized economy will suffice to replace the need for development cooperation. If countries would just adopt trade and investment-friendly policies, and make other structural adjustments, they could hitch their wagons to the global economy and enjoy the ride to greater prosperity — or so the myth has it.

But we know that these approaches are no answer for much of the developing world. There is no correlation between need and direct foreign investment. Almost all direct foreign investment goes to 20 countries; just 6 per cent to Africa, and 2 per cent to the LDCs.

Moreover, even where we have the right macro framework and fundamentals in place, with growing privatization, liberalization and deregulation, the result is often growth, but growth with increasing poverty, increasing inequity, increasing unemployment. Too much emphasis on macro policy alone has led to lopsided, unbalanced and environmentally unsustainable growth.

So, any credible attack on world poverty must include a large increase in development cooperation — and some increase in development assistance — at its core. Our world today needs more development assistance, not less, whatever the trend might be. But the need is for radically reformed development cooperation:

· cooperation which recognizes that economies exist for people, not people for economies;

· cooperation that benefits the poor more than the rich;

· cooperation not just with governments but with the private sector and civil society;

· cooperation that recognizes that the polity is as important as the economy;

· cooperation that seeks growth, yes, but growth with equity, empowerment, employment and environment;

· cooperation that no longer gives more to high military spenders than to low military spenders;

· cooperation, in effect, for sustainable human development.

We at the United Nations — under the Secretary-General's leadership — have concluded that the attack on poverty should have five principal thrusts — five thrusts that come directly from the continuum of the United Nations conferences which began at Rio, continued most recently at Beijing, and will conclude with Habitat II and the World Food Summit this year.

The good news is that, for the first time, we have the entire United Nations system, including the Bretton Woods institutions, organized around these five thrusts:

First basic social services for all — investing in people — led by UNFPA and including UNICEF, WHO, UNESCO and others;

Second jobs and sustainable livelihoods for all, led by ILO and including UNDP, UNIDO, and others. Food security is included here, and so are the UN's three Rome-based agricultural agencies;

Third sustaining the natural resource base, on which the poor depend. Here, the machinery put in place to follow-up on the 1992 Rio Earth Summit continues;

Fourth advancement and empowerment of women, led by the Secretary-General's special representative on gender issues, with support from UNIFEM and others; and

Fifth the overarching "enabling environment", led by the World Bank and including UNCTAD, IMF, UNDP and others.

Among other tasks, the enabling environment group is focusing on good governance. The UNDP role in this area has increased. One-third of UNDP resources are now being spent on capacity development for good governance.

The enabling environment is extraordinarily important. We must make trade work for the poor, not against them; we must make direct foreign investment correlate with need; the burdens of structural adjustment must be made to fall more on the rich than on the poor; and growth must be equitably and fairly distributed.

In all five areas, United Nations Headquarters groups are working to support United Nations country teams. At the country level, Resident Coordinators are organizing United Nations teams around these themes.

The Group of 77 has offered to work with us on this follow-up to United Nations conferences, and we accept. The OECD/DAC has also supported this approach.

UNDP has been given responsibility for integrating all this into concerted support for country-led, country-owned, country-devised anti-poverty plans of action.

In this era of cries for United Nations reform, this is a major United Nations reform.

None of this will be possible without a revitalized commitment to an enlarged but reformed development cooperation.

Is it not ironic that:

· just when the cold war is over and we are freed at last from its distorting effects on development assistance;

· just when we know so much more about what works and what does not work in development cooperation; and

· just when the needs of the developing world for assistance are growing;

that right at this crucial juncture, support for cooperation is declining?

And it is not ironic that:

· just when we have greater understanding of the powerful link between peace and development, seen by all in the Middle East and Bosnia;

· just when the United Nations is called upon to assume even greater responsibilities, including the follow-up to UN conferences and post-conflict peace-building; and

· just when the United Nations is reforming itself to do these things;

that right at this crucial juncture, financial resources available to the UN to do these things are declining?

Yes, there are ironies; but they are more, they are tragedies. Let us not doubt it.

Let it not be said of our time that we, who had the power to do better, allowed the world to become further divided. We need cohesive globalization, not fractured globalization. Together, let us build a new World Wide Web — a World Wide Web of equity, hope and opportunity.
