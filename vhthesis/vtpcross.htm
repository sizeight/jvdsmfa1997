3. THE CROSSING PROJECT

3.1. Description

The Crossing Project was a virtual environment created by Stacey Spiegel in collaboration with Rodney Hoinkes, presented at the Rotterdam harbour simulator, as part of DEAF 95 -- 'The Dutch Electronic Art Festival'. The idea behind the Crossing Project was that virtual agents collect data on a particular subject on the world wide web. This information was then used as data to 'grow' or 'build' a virtual environment.

What immediately appealed to me about this concept, was that one could apply any data as a source for virtual growth. The social issues that the implementation of information technology implied -- touched upon in my initial research -- could be visualized in dramatic and spectacular fashion. The harbour simulator, situated in Rotterdam (arguably the biggest export harbour of the world) and utilizing some of the most advanced computer simulation technology, became a very powerful symbol for me of the Western world’s adventure into the world of cyberspace and its position towards the rest of the world -- 'left behind in reality'. At a time of unequalled wealth disparity, never made visible so clearly because of the global communications revolution, the challenge was how to make a powerful artistic statement using this facility. The Crossing Project provided some answers...

3.2. Concept

"Crossings is focused on the discourse surrounding the mechanization of nature. Its environment is the signature of our post-industrial era and reasserts that both reality and virtuality are constructs that extend beyond the geographical terrain into the conceptual realm." (Spiegel 1997) [10] [Figure 8]

Figure 8: Crossings installation, May 12-21, 1995,
ZKM's Multimediale, Karlsruhe, Germany
(Spiegel 1997)

As an alternative method to navigate the information space of cyberspace, Spiegel and Hoinkes' "basic assumption is that, by translating experience from the real world, virtual landscapes become primary opportunities to explore cultural identity and play with the nature of information." Spiegel explains that reconstructing a simulated 3-dimensional landscape, this "metaphor for real space -- dimensionally accurate, though abstracted in representation -- extends the surreal potential meaning through landscape." Spiegel and Hoinkes are playing at the boundary of representation. Although their discourse takes place in cyberspace it is their belief that all the environments they create are visual analogues of the 'real' 3-dimensional world we live in. (Spiegel 1997) [Figure 9]

Figure 9: Crossings installation, May 12-21, 1995,
ZKM's Multimediale, Karlsruhe, Germany
(Spiegel 1997)

"As one roams around, the symbolic layers of the environment unfold into psychological, emotional and intellectual spaces. Like Alice passing through the looking-glass, each form one encounters in the landscape has the potential to link the participant to other realities." (Spiegel 1997)

3.3 Experience

In practice the idea was that hypermedia links allow the viewer to access sound, text and images as well as animations and video available on the world wide web. Having experienced the installation myself, this aspect of the installation did not seem to be implemented at the time.

However, what did excite me very much about this installation was the element of "playing at the boundary of representation." As viewer one was standing on a moving platform (the pilothouse) that could be 'navigated' through the 360° surround computer generated landscape created by Spiegel and Hoinkes. Experiencing this with a group of other people that reacted to situations unfolding and sounds coming from the virtual landscape contributed very much to the overall sensation.

The absence of bulky and inconvenient VR headgear, allowing for only a solo experience into virtual reality, appealed very much to me. The pace of the movements (being a ship simulator) were also very smooth and almost dreamlike, thereby enhancing the surreal feeling. Unlike any virtual reality worlds experienced in game arcades and Disneyworld attractions before, this 'show' really felt like a journey into someone else's imagination, but then accompanied by a group of people -- talking, laughing and discussing our shared experience.

3.4 Technical

Two primary software packages, PolyTRIM [Figure 10], a real-time design inquiry program, and CLRMosaic [Figure 11], a simplified version of Polytrim that is integrated with the Mosaic web browser were used by Spiegel and Hoinkes. Models and environments have been developed in PolyTRIM, AutoCAD and other modellers (incorporated through DXF file format), automated algorithms, and C-library additions. Filters also exist from many GIS (Geographic Information Systems) and CAD (DXF, SGO, and Architrion) systems or can be generated directly into SDML format.

Figure 10: PolyTRIM Software; Polygonal Toolkit for
Representation, Interaction and Modelling

"PolyTRIM is a research toolkit for the exploration of design and planning issues at both a pedagogical and professional level. It is premised around the ideas of interaction with design ideas and constructs to allow investigation at an intuitive speed, and the facilitation of more democratic design processes through participatory workshop strategies." (PolyTRIM 1997) [11]

Figure 11: CLRMosaic; Distributed Spatial Data Navigator.
(CLRMosaic 1997)

"CLRMosaic is a World-Wide Web client, allowing the handling of spatial databases along with traditional hypertext data sources. This provides a mechanism for people to provide hooks to resources that are fundamentally spatially organized, geographic mapping, 3d reconstructions, architectural proposals, cyberspaces, etc. This client is premised around the notion of interactive exploration of environments and easy access to information through it." ( CLRMosaic 1997) [12]
