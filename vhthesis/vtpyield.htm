5.YIELD

5.1. Description

The central objective of Yield (computer animation and installation) [Figure 16] was to develop the iconography of The Virtual Harvester project and to test the design credibility of the digital models. Because both productions, Yield and Data Field (to be described later), make use of the same 3D digital information, it creates the possibility for one project to grow into the other. Portraying a more oppressive vision of our digital world, I have set out to project an environment wherein the technologies of freedom and information threaten to turn into machines of dominance and control. "While the tourist hovers past in his private helicopter / we are down here / trying to figure out / what will be the weapon / that we will one day wield." (Groenewegen 1997) [16]

Figure 16: Yield installation, Ziegler Gallery Groningen
10 February - 2 March 1997

5.2. Cyberpunk

The iconography of Yield, developing from the research done for An Electronic Laager (as described earlier), was influenced by the writings of the cyberpunk movement in '80s science fiction (SF). Although cyberpunk is the product of the 1980's milieu, it is rooted in the tradition of popular SF literature, especially those with a more dystopian message. Of special importance are the '60s and '70s New Wave writers such as Samuel R. Delany, Philip K. Dick, and J. G. Ballard, as well as the postmodern fragmentations of William Burroughs and apocalypticism of Thomas Pynchon.

Figure 17: Mel Gibson in Mad Max Beyond the Thunderdome, 1985.
(Sharret 1993: 257)

Also influential were the "postmodern" movies of the last decade (TRON, Blade Runner, the Mad Max films, [Figure 17] Brazil, Robocop, [Figure 18] Terminator 2, The Lawnmower Man etc.), portraying material conditions at the end of the century in a catastrophic state of decline. Other noted influences are musicians and punk bands (Laurie Anderson, Lou Reed and the Velvet Underground, Sonic Youth, The Sex Pistols, Pink Floyd, The Clash, U2), and performance and installation artists (Nam June Paik, Demers and Vorn, Stelarc, Mark Pauline and the Survival Research Laboratories). Unlike the Star Wars and Indiana Jones movies, with their romantic reaffirmation of the righteous empire and neo-colonial adventurism, the spaces described in cyberpunk fiction were not the expansive realms of far-away empires or enormous spaceships. Cyberpunk rather described a street level science fiction that foregrounded a perverse urbanism, marked by its visual intensity. [17]

This highly-stylized narrative form can be read as a reflection of the information overload that characterizes today's urban societies, and McLuhan's metaphor of the implosion of the media. (Expansive SF was based on historical analogies of colonialism and exploration.) The implosive SF of cyberpunk is based on analogies of the invasion and transformation of the body (prosthetic limbs, cosmetic surgery) and mind (brain-computer interfaces, neuro-chemistry) by the 'alien' technologies of our own making. It draws from the history of the development of robotics and computers as Manuel De Landa describes: "In this chart the robot would see the evolution of armies as machines (clockworks, motors and networks), the different forms in which intelligence 'migrated' from human bodies to become incarnated in physical contraptions, and the processes through which artificial forms of perception (vision, hearing) came to be synthesized and embodied in computers." (1991: 10)

Cyberpunk unveils how these technologies are redefining the nature of humanity and the nature of self as the distinction between organic and non-organic life is becoming blurred. From these writers point of view humans have served only as "machines' surrogate reproductive organs until robots acquired their own self-replication capabilities." (1991: 7) The cyberpunk artists' vision is that the technological dreams and nightmares spoken of in SF literature are already part of reality. A collapse of the future on the present has occurred with the advent of post-industrialism.

Figure 18: Robocop , 1987. Directed by Paul Verhoeven. (Bukatman 1993: 309)

5.3. Wasteland

In his provocative book (which I will refer to extensively during this study) The Ends of the Earth: A journey to the frontiers of Anarchy reporter Robert Kaplan relates a story he was told by a foreign ambassador while he dined with him in Abidjan. I am referring to this account because it sounds like a description from a modern cyberpunk novel and hence it relates very well to the helicopter sequence in Yield. [Figure 19] In these scenes a helicopter transports the viewers across a virtual wasteland towards lush 'data fields of poverty'.

The ambassador told about a visit he had made to the extreme northwest of the Ivory Coast to observe an international aid project supported by the United Nations, implemented to eradicate river blindness. Black flies accumulating over and alongside the rivers of interior Africa causes the disease when they sting African people as they bathe and wash their clothes in the rivers.

Figure 19: The helicopter sequence in Yield, 1997.

It was a difficult and dangerous operation. The helicopters doing the spraying had to deal with flying upwind at high speeds, near the water, over and in between small side streams not much higher than their rotor blades, with trees on either side. Landsat satellites beamed down computerized pictures showing the state of each river and side stream. Highly sensitive computers monitored the chemicals content of the river water, requiring constant readjustments in the ratio of the six chemical comprising the spray repellent. The pilots came from the U.S., Canada, Peru, Portugal, and the former Yugoslavia. The ambassador told Kaplan how impressed he was by their military discipline.

Though the pilots succeed in eradicating river blindness from the immediate region, the disease could easily return to nearby Liberia and Nigeria if the project was ever ended. "This is what it takes," said the ambassador, full of awe. "The highest levels of technology, attention to the finest details, and the most extraordinary talent and work ethic" are required to halt one disease, in one part of Africa, temporarily. The ambassador went on to describe how the pilots had been rising before dawn every day for a few years to be briefed and to study their flight plans and computer printouts. Western food was flown from Abidjan and trucked to their compounds. The pilots had their own generators for electricity. The whole set-up was like a base on the moon. (Kaplan 1996: 87-88)

5.4. Virtual Reality

In their imaginations they saw the cinema as the total and complete representation of reality; they saw in a trice the reconstruction of a perfect illusion of the outside world in sound, color and relief. - André Bazin, 1946 (Bukatman 1993: 77)

But in the computer country, they dream of eliminating the interface, of breaking down the barrier of the screen and eliminating the distinctions made between this side and that side. - Karrie Jacobs, 1990 (Bukatman 1993: 77)

Virtual reality (VR) [18] technologies have attracted much attention at the beginning of the 1990's in attempting to simulate cyberspace. At this stage, the talk surrounding the 'immersive interface' of virtual reality far outstrips the achievements. The VR apparatus, bulky and inconvenient, is far from the desired ideal of a 'transparent interface.' At the same time the computational requirements to run these systems are so intense as to put the experience out of reach for all but the wealthiest, such as the military and entertainment clients. However, it can be assumed that the technology will improve to the point of widespread feasibility and affordability within a decade.

Yet, the mere existence of virtual reality raises important questions. "Such ontological and epistemological structures as the nature of the human, the real, experience, sensation, cognition, identity and gender are all placed, if not under erasure then certainly in question around the (still largely) discursive object of virtual reality and the postulated existence of perfect, simulated, environment. Virtual reality has become the very embodiment of postmodern disembodiment." (Bukatman 1993: 78)

For me virtual reality becomes a powerful metaphor by which to question the Western world's isolation from the social and economic realities of the rest of the developing world. As the helicopter story in the previous section illustrates, the Western world's involvement with places like Africa is becoming more and more detached and disembodied, not unlike an exploration into cyberspace.

5.5. Cyberspace

What the implosive SF of William Gibson's book Neuromancer (1984) deals with is the invisible spaces of electronic circulation or microscopic nanotechnologies. As such cyberpunk is concerned with the intimacy of the contemporary interfaces that exist between human subjects and electronic technology. [Figure 20]

Figure 20: "The cybersubjectivities of virtual reality,"
TRON. (Sharrett 1993: 77)

In cyberpunk literature 'urban space' depicts more than just a realistic milieu in which the punk protagonists fight out their battles. It is the central metaphor used to describe the phenomenologically relevant 'other space' of information circulation and data control of today's electronic culture, coined by Gibson as cyberspace. "Urban space and cyberspace become reciprocal metaphors – each enables an understanding and negotiation of the other." (Bukatman 1993: 145) Gibson's defined cyberspace as a consensual hallucination -- a software meditation -- whose function is precisely to provide that vision of, and entry into, the invisible -- but actual -- world within the computer.

Cyberspace is thus an abstraction that provides a narrative compensation of McLuhan's idea of the loss of visibility and space in the technological, cybernetic world of today. It is a metaphor attempting to recognize and overcome the technological estrangements of the electronic age. In this way, cyberpunk becomes the defining movement / element of contemporary society. Indeed, as Frederick Jameson puts it, "cyberpunk is the supreme literary expression, if not of postmodernism, then of late capitalism itself". (Jameson 1991: 419)

5.6. Design

"Sometimes the design is the statement." -- Ridley Scott, director of Blade Runner. (Sammon 1996) [Figure 21]

Figure 21: "Deckard attempts to leap between two roofs..."
Blade Runner, 1982. Directed by Ridley Scott, (Sammon 1996: 44)

For the styling and design of Yield, I deliberately set out to portray the banal 'hyper-reality' of the electronic 'cyberspace' within the journey towards the data fields takes place. Unlike big-budget Hollywood productions of the '90s (Jurrasic Park, Speed, Titanic), wherein it becomes impossible to discern computer generated images (CGI) from 'real' footage, Yield draws from '80s films like TRON [Figure 22] and The Last Starfighter. TRON was the first commercial film to rely on entirely computer generated images and sequences. These effects were characteristically too perfect to be accepted as 'facsimiles' of the real world -- the surfaces were too smooth and undetailed, the reflections were perfectly focussed, movements were unswerving and non-inertial. The perfect coordinate space of the terminal field allowed a precision of perspective that could not escape the ordinary eye. "The body, with its analogic imperfections, is exempted from this digital world -- contra TRON's narrative strategies, this is precisely a disembodied space." (Bukatman1993: 88)

The slick, perfectly stylized machines appearing in Yield, stood in sharp contrast to the organic, worked shapes and surfaces of my sculptural objects. As one friend remarked: "...I just miss the absolute raw power that is so present in your sculptures -- rough hewn stone and welded steel. Your threeD [sic] world is very clean and antiseptic."
