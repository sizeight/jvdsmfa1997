8.CONCLUSION

In the introduction of this dissertation I stated "that I am not only reflecting on the society in which I live, but am also trying to extend the possibilities opened up for sculpture by new electronic technologies. In the same way that cubism, via industrial society, altered the course of sculpture, I suggested that cybernetics / cyberpunk, via the electronic / information society, is leading to a new direction in sculpture."

During my studies at MEDIA-GN, I have explored how the combination of computer animation / simulation with data influx from the world wide web can be applied to art projects, specifically towards creating interactive virtual environments. After this exploration, my current view regarding sculpture can best be summed up by an answer given by Greg Lynn in an interview at "The Roelof Kiers Masterclass" [38]. When asked how he sees media changing architecture, Lynn, an architectural theorist, designer and professor at Columbia University, responded: "Architecture often thinks of itself as a very material discipline. While in fact, most of what we do is transmitted in a mediaform like drawings and computerfiles. Media makes architecture seen [sic] less about matter and a little more about information." (1997)

Initially media effects design (and thus sculpture) in a twofold way by generative design and community design. Firstly, the growing availability of cheap and powerful computational power, offers a range of new design techniques. Using computers as a generative tool, the possibility of involving large quantities of variables allow to derive more complex spatial configurations. (Hensel 1997) The future of sculpture seems to lie in the 'breeding' and evolving of form, the work of Karl Sims serving as an example. In Yield this is generated by the multiplied versions of the cornplant. Starting with a single digital modelled maize plant, I could quickly create a cornfield.

Secondly, advanced telecommunications and networking enable the immediate integration of several groups of participants into the design process via direct access to the project's dataset. Through continual available access in the development of projects, clients and specialist consultants can partake in the design process simultaneously. (Hensel 1997) This highly flexible system was applied in the production of the maize plant and combiner 3D models for Yield. All mediation taking place via email, these computer models were specialy constructed for the project in Madrid [39], and also delivered via email once completed.

Furthermore, I am interested in how projects like the Virtual Harvester can be used to generate "media events," giving rise to popular awareness of the use of the world wide web and its capacity as an inexpensive distribution network. This communication medium has enormous potential to implement and affect development programs around the world. One such project is the comic strip series Benza and Betty, drawn by my friend Jacques Coetzer for the Agriculture Research Council (ARC) in South Africa. The project aims to facilitate practical agriculture-related information to developing communities in sub-Saharan Africa. [40] Green Dish – an ongoing undertaking, is a network of people involved in the implementation of digital media and information technology in the developing world.

The Virtual Harvester is a project incorporating art and technology, striving to show how simple interaction by a few people in one part of the globe can have a tremendous impact on the lives of many people elsewhere. As such, the symbol of the "gieter" (watering-can) is an apt metaphor signifying the importance of interactive involvement. "Poverty places an intolerable burden on the human spirit. We cannot accept that it will always be with us. The modern world has the resources, the know-how and the expertise to relegate poverty to the pages of history." (James Gustave Speth, Administrator, UNDP International Day for the Eradication of Poverty, 17-10-1996)
