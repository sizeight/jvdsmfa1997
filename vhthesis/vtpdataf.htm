6.DATA FIELD

6.1. Description

Data Field is based on the original proposal for The Reality of Virtual Reality Project -- ISEA 96. During this symposium the large harbour simulator of MarineSafety Rotterdam [Figure 23] was made available for artistic projects. The simulator consists of 360° visual, 3D sound and a moving platform (the pilothouse) with two degrees of freedom. The computer system consists of 22 SGI Crimsons and a DEC Vax. MarineSafety International [19] is a wholly-owned subsidiary of FlightSafety International, providing simulator training for Marine pilots and Naval officers. Other MSI centres are located in Kings Point, NY; Newport, RI; San Diego, CA and Valejo,CA. ( MarineSafety 1997)

Figure 23: The large harbour simulator of MarineSafety Rotterdam.
(MarineSafety 1997)

Data Field was conceptualized to be experienced in one of the harbour simulator facilities of MarineSafety International. Instead of a ship’s bridge, the platform will become a fictional harvester-cabin (wherein groups of 10 -- 15 people can stand). In front of the viewers, virtually attached to the pilothouse, will be an enormous virtual harvesting device. This device will make a loud mechanical noise (amplified by the harbour simulators 3D sound), vibrating the pilothouse from side to side whilst reaping the harvest. At "7 o'clock" in relation to the front of the pilothouse vision, there will be a virtual pipe protruding into space from which the virtual grains are blown into virtual storage trucks moving slowly alongside the pilothouse. Rising above the noise and mechanical activity, the viewers will become aware of the scent of fresh grain. The grain odour will rise from a thick layer of yellow maize, covering the entire inside floor area of the simulator.

The group standing, protected inside the pilothouse, will be able to steer the virtual combine through green virtual maize fields, [Figure 24] leaving a trail of scorched earth behind the harvester's reaping blades. Being able to control the movements of the harvester, the group can choose between several corn fields ripe for reaping, surrounding them in virtual space. Protruding from amongst the maize fields are gigantic communication towers serving as beacons amidst the vast virtual landscape. While the group 'plays the game,' it is subtly revealed (via incoming messages on the harvester's two-way radio system) that the virtual corn fields they are busy reaping are in fact 3D visual data representation fields. The corn fields they are surrounded by represent the number of people living in absolute poverty across the globe...

Hence, each corn plant in the corn field represents one person living in poverty. The number of people living in poverty, and thus the number of corn plants, is based on the statistics of the United Nations "Poverty Clock".

Figure 24: "A pseudo-reconciliation of generation, moral lapses, and ideology."
Kevin Costner in Field of Dreams, 1989.
(Sharrett 1993: 89)

As the viewers leave the pilothouse, each person will receive a plastic chip card, as a reminder of their virtual experience within the data field (of poverty). The chip on this card will contain information about the amount of corn they, as a group, have harvested in their session and the number of plants that still remains unharvested. Such a card can give access to the Virtual Harvester's central data base. This data base can be accessed by way of the global automatic banking network. In this way, a current statement on the virtual corn field’s growth can be given 24 hours a day.

Apart from referring to virtual reality and cyberspace (as described in the previous section), Data Field incorporates four other related aspects of today’s information age in its application as an installation. They are feedback and cybernetics, networks, simulation and hyperreality.

6.2. Feedback and Cybernetics

Ktesisbios who lived in Alexandria in the first half of the third century B.C. is credited today for inventing the first 'true' automatic device. Ktesisbios's clock [20] kept extremely good time by self-regulating its water supply. The regulating valve that he invented to compromize the water flow was a predecessor to the floating ball in the upper chamber of today's flush toilet. After a flush, the floating ball goes down with the declining water level, pulling open the water supply valve. The incoming water fills the chamber again, raising the ball so that its arm closes the flow of water at the precise level assigned to it. As such the working of a flush toilet is a perfect example to describe the principle of a feedback loop.

The flyball regulator [21] installed onto steam engines regulating the engine to run at any rate was a revolutionary feedback mechanism that had a profound influence on the industrial revolution. The steam engines that ran the mills of the industrial age would have been unworkable creations (exploding by their own force) if it wasn't for the parallel information revolution, launched by the automatic feedback system that the flyball regulator enforced. "So information, and not coal itself, turned the power of machines useful and therefore more desirable." (Kelly 1995:115) Thus the feedback mechanisms inherent in the workings of steam engines of the industrial revolution pointed the way towards the information age of our present post-industrial society.

Norbert Wiener, wrote: "Feedback is a method of controlling a system by reinserting into it the results of its past performance."(Kelly 1995: 439) Wiener, an American mathematician, coined the word 'cybernetics' in 1947 to describe the idea of feedback and servomechanics. As the title of his book implies, Cybernetics: or control and communication in the animal and the machine, for Wiener cybernetics comprised all systems, mechanical, electronic and biological, in which the flow of information played a role. Wiener used 'cybernetic' to describe the concept of machines that can handle information, make decisions, and control the operation of other machines. Because the concept of information plays such a central role, cybernetics is also known as 'information theory' or 'communication theory'. As such the science of cybernetics was the forerunner of modern computer science. [22]

Figure 25: A graphic description of a feedback loop.
I - input instruction; E - initial execution; D - outside influence;
F - feedback from outside influence; O - actual execution.
(Gérardin 1968: 48)

The aim of cybernetic analysis is to define and isolate which particular piece of information passing between parts of a system motivates which action, or alters which states in the communicants. In its most basic form, the cybernetic system is made up of a receptor obtaining information as input from the outside. This information is processed and sent to an effector or sender which then responds to the initial information with output. The output, however, alters the original conditions on the outside, thus leading to new information being sent in the form of input back into the system, establishing what is called a 'feedback loop'. [Figure 25]

By extension, cybernetics can be applied to the study of any communication system. These include information processing mechanisms such as computers, self-governing machines and automata, and communication systems inherent within the human body such as the relationship between brain and behaviour. Because of these applications, cybernetics is an appropriate metaphor for conceptualising society: the notion that the brain functions like a computer, that a person is like a machine, that society is like a feedback mechanism, allows for an investigation into the ways in which information functions in a post-industrial age.

In An Electronic Laager I reflected on the ways the National Party had used information for indoctrination purposes in apartheid era South Africa. I expanded the theme so as to question the freedom of individuals in the configuration of the mass media and state apparatuses that form the "neocapitalist cybernetic order."(Baudrillard 1983: 111) [23] [Figure 26]

Figure 26: A police "Casspir" driving through a squatter township near Cape Town.
(The Argus Newspaper 1994, February 2)

The aim of the Virtual Harvester project is to simulate the growth of poverty in the world today, synchronized to input from the UNDP's Poverty Clock. Immersed into 'data fields of poverty' the group of participants seem to have no alternative but to harvest more poverty, or alternatively to stop the machine. As such the project reflects on the disparity between the rich and the poor which is growing wider in today’s global village, with no obvious solution at hand. A feedback loop is thus in place that can lead to only one result -- anarchy.

6.3. Networks

Described as the largest functioning anarchy in the world, the Internet links several millions of personal computers around the world. [24] Kevin Kelly wrote in Out of Control: "The symbol of science for the next century is the dynamical Net." (Kelly 1994: 25) The Net is an archetype to represent circuits, interdependence, large economics, social and ecological systems. There is hardly a single aspect in the world of business that has not been influenced either directly or indirectly by the introduction of "network logic". [25] [Figure 27] Networks allows companies to manufacture new kinds of products, in faster and more flexible ways, in greater response to customers needs, in much shorter time. Laws and the economy have changed due to global 24-hour networking of financial institutions. Instant cash from ATM machines, credit cards, international travel, fax machines, email, internet, etc. are all heavily reliant on networks.

Figure 27: "FedEx is piloting the next phase of the Digital Revolution.
It's a very big idea. It's called logistics." (Lappin 1996)

Some of the characteristics to describe networks are distributed (no single location), decentralized (no central control), collaborative (mutual benefit), adaptive (work in realtime -- bar codes, laser scanners, cellular phones), flexibility (smaller numbers of items in smaller time periods), mass customization (individuality), global accounting (economic frontiers disappear) and increasing returns (them that has, gets more).

The first example of a distributed network implemented by humans was the Nazi Blitzkrieg: a network of weapon systems integrated together through the use of radio communications. As such it showed the way to the future in warfare, where the emphasis switched from machines with components in physical contact with each other to machines with components operating over vast geographical distances. (De Landa 1991: 150 and158) Influenced by this historical information the machines appearing in the Virtual Harvester, painted in the green and yellow livery of the state or 'organisation', are portrayed as being in constant communication with each other.

6.4. Simulation

"SimCity [26] is an urban theory provided with a users interface. In the same sense, a dollhouse is a theory of the household. A novel is a theory told as story. A flight simulator is an interactive theory of aviation." Kelly goes on to say "a theory abstracts the complicated pattern of real things into the facsimile pattern -- a model, or a simulation...If done well, the miniature captures some integrity of the larger hole." (Kelly 1994: 236)

The modern era of computer simulated war games began in the 1950s at the RAND Corporation. Today on an everyday basis at a dozen U.S. army bases around the world, tank and aircraft pilots compete with each other in simulated AirLand battles connected to each other by a military system called SIMNET. As Douglas Nelms, National Defence columnist explains, SIMNET "transports crews of land and aerial vehicles from planet Earth to a surrogate world where they can do battle without the constraints of safety, cost, environmental impact or geographical boundaries." (Kelly 1994: 244) One tank can attack another tank within the virtual land of SIMNET, even though some of the crew may be in a simulator in California while others could be in a simulator in Germany. Long-haul telephone lines link the other 300 existing simulator boxes worldwide into one network, enabling them all to move through the same virtual battleground.

What the US military is working towards is simulated training so 'real' that it becomes indistinguishable from actual combat. Already a real human tank gunner operator’s only portal to the outside battlefield is through a tiny TV display in front of his face; his link to the rest of his crew is through a headset. For all practical purposes a real gunner in a real tank operates a simulation. This slipperiness between real life and virtual life can be described by the term hyperreality.

6.5. Hyperreality

Figure 28: An attack on a hydro electric plant,
Iraq, broadcast on CNN, 21 January 1991.
(Wombell 1991: 101)

The Italian novelist Umberto Eco, driving around America in the early 1970's, found it a country bustling in messages about simulation and degrees of reality. The national icon, Coca-Cola advertised itself as "the real thing." Eco wrote that he was on a "journey into hyperreality, in search of instances where the American imagination demands the real thing and, to attain it, must fabricate the absolute fake.”(Kelly 1995:238) Hyperreality thus emerges where "absolute unreality is offered as real presence."(1995:238).

In his book Simulations (1983) the French philosopher Jean Baudrillard states: "Abstraction today is no longer that of the map, the double, the mirror or the concept. Simulation is no longer that of a territory, a reverential being, or a substance. It is the generation of models of a real without origin or reality: a hyperreal."(1995:239) A very good computer simulation, like the one I would like to create in the large harbour simulator of Rotterdam, is thus a perfect example of hyperreality.

A more bizarre example of hyper-reality was the coverage of the Gulf War by CNN. [Figure 28] Television audiences across the world seemed to have had a similar view of the battle as the participants. Commenting on the war Baudrillard stated: "Our strategic site is the television screen, from which we are bombarded". He argued that there was no "thing itself","no real war depicted in the images", but only a simulation of it. (Woolley 1993: 196)

6.6. Production

The 3D models used in Yield will be incorporated into the computer systems of MarineSafety as DFX [27] files. I spoke to Mr. Wim van Eck, Technical Manager, MarineSafety Rotterdam, at the time of Spiegel and Hoinkes Safe Harbour [28] installation during the DEAF 96: Digital Territories symposium. He took me on an tour through the MarineSafety facility, showing me also the smaller simulator environments available at the facility. He then explained to me that the spatial orientation and principles of the software used by the harbour facility are based on the Cartesian coordinate system, similar to the software I used to produce Yield. [Figure 29] This implies that the learning curve to familiarize oneself with the 3D software used in the harbour simulator should be short.

Figure 29: 3D Studio Max is a three-dimensional modeling and animation software programme
designed for the Windows NT operating system, running on PC.

A specialised computer programming unit such as MEDIA-GN research and development (who gave system support for the Yield production) will be consulted to do the platform independent C / C++ programming, relating to the manipulation and interactivity of 3D models. It is foreseen that the integration with the harbour simulator's computer system can best be done by the programmers at MarineSafety, Rotterdam. An independent musician (as was the case for the music produced for Yield) will be consulted as sound engineer to create the Dolby Surround / 3D sound for the project.
