2. INITIAL RESEARCH

Having started my studies in The Netherlands I immediately set out to investigate and develop possible art projects. I wanted to come up with projects that would combine the research and exploration of new digital information technology with my iconography as developed during my work and studies in South Africa. My approach as an artist, influenced by the period I have worked as assistant to the South African sculptor David Brown [Figure 4] in Cape Town, is that a certain thread of thought should always be present in my work. Working in different mediums could bring forth a lot of different styles and methods, but the iconography informing them all should remain constant. This approach taught by Brown, who in turn was influenced by the work of the South African painter and graphic artist Cecil Skotnes, stems from a long sculptural tradition as can be seen in the work of o.a. Henry Moore, David Smith, and Constantin Brancusi.

2.1. Mechatronics

As an artist my interest in 'mechatronics' [7] -- the synergetic combination of traditional engineering disciplines such as mechanics, electronics and computer science whose implementation is most obvious in the field of robotics -- is twofold. First, it is practical, relating to the field of constructed metal and bronze sculpture as well as dealing with cybernetics and artificial intelligence, and second, it is theoretical and conceptual, considering the issues that the use and implementation of these 'robots' expose.

2.2. From Sculpture to Mechatronics

Through the method of collage the cubists incorporated products of industrial mass production into their work. These processes in turn had an influence on the development of modern sculpture, seen in Picasso's use of tin in the production of constructions such as Guitar of 1912. From that moment, sculpture was not conceived of as a solid mass, but rather as an open construction of planes. From the Guitar, via Russian constructivism, seen in the work of Vladimir Tatlin, a tradition of assembled metal sculpture was inaugurated in the West which culminated in the work of David Smith and Anthony Caro.

For an artist like Smith the use of metal counted for more than merely the radical new possibilities it opened up for sculpture: it symbolised the industrial age to which he belonged. "My aim", he wrote in 1952, "is the same as in locomotive building: to arrive at a given functional form in the most efficient manner". (Van der Schijff: 1996) [Figure 6]

Figure 6: David Smith. Spoleto, Italy, 1963.
(Chipp 1968)

For me as an artist, working in the area of computer graphics, one growing concern and point of frustration is how to overcome the limitations of interacting with a computer screen, working entirely in the virtual world, and to move back into the organic, chaotic, 'real' physical world in which we find ourselves immersed most of the time and whose tactile qualities are so important to me as a sculptor.

Serendipity may have offered me an outcome to my dilemma. While sourcing references in the library of the Gasunie's [8] head office in Groningen to describe the MIG -- Metal Inert Gas Welding Process, a welding method I used extensively to construct sculptures, I came upon the journal of the International Institute of Welding, Welding in the World. With the theme "Advanced Techniques and Low Cost Automation," 1994, Volume 34, it gave a survey of recent advances and practical applications on mechatronics and robotics in modern welding engineering. [9] Although I was aware of the use of robots in car production and shipbuilding, I had never heard of 'mechatronics' or considered the application thereof in artistic / sculptural terms. However, the more I read about the introduction of advanced robotics and information technology into modern welding fabrication, the more excited I became about the possibilities it contained for making sculpture and interactive installations. [Figure 7]

Figure 7: With CAD programming systems, down times of manufacturing units due to programming can be reduced considerably. (Hicks 1994: 92)

All along it seems that the indicators, of the path sculpture could follow within the new paradigm of information technology, were available within the welding techniques I used and applied everyday in the studio for seven years, while practising as a sculptor. My knowledge of welding, combined with the 3D computer animation experience I have gained over the last two years, gives me the confidence to seriously consider the field of mechatronics and especially the application of robotics in modern welding, as a subject for research underlying my main project at MDEIA-GN. The final outcome of the project can take many forms; from an exhibit / installation making use of actual welding robots, to better interface design for the software used in running these robotic systems.

I eventually decided not to proceed with this project after discussing my ideas with Prof. dr. ir. A.C.H. Van der Wolf, Universitair Technologisch Instituut voor Integrale Produkt en Produktie Vernieuwing / Mechatronica, Technische Universiteit Eindhoven -- T.U.E. He advised me that the idea was probably to complex for me to realize with the technical knowledge at my disposal at that stage. It is my desire that sometime in the future a project incorporating some of these ideas and technologies might still be realized. However, the research done for Is there a link between Modern Sculpture and Mechatronics ? planted a seed that would come to sprout while experiencing the Crossing Project.
